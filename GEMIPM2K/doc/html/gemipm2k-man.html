1. Introduction

Modern approaches to reactive mass transport (RMT) or fluid mass transport (FMT) modelling require simultaneous account for mass transport of contaminants through aqueous and/or gas phase in subsurface, and their interaction with wall-rock minerals, including (ad)sorption, (co)precipitation,  and mineral dissolution.  These chemical processes depend on redox state of the system, are inherently complex, and may involve many phases and chemical species with kinetically controlled interactions. They may indirectly affect the mass transport via porosity and permeability changes due to mineral dissolution or precipitation.

Direct inclusion of all possible chemical reactions in the mass transport model often renders it intractable in 2D or 3D space. If this is the case, the chemical model must be dramatically simplified to a few reactions, which puts its chemical plausibility under question.

An alternative approach is to keep the full-complexity chemical model solver in a kind of "black box" which can be applied separately to each node of the FMT model at a given time point.  The  input of the "chemical black box" at time t  is just the bulk chemical composition b of the reactive part of the node, as well as temperature T, pressure P, and optionally, kinetic constraints k on some reactions or amounts of certain components. Some or all these inputs could have been changed by the mass transport part on its previous iteration time step t-1 over all nodes.  Now, the "chemical black box" equilibration is applied to each node independently at time t, to produce changed speciation n (amounts or concentrations of chemical species in all reactive phases),  new phase volumes, redox (Eh, pe of aqueous solution, gas fugacities), chemical potentials of components, etc. This procedure can also detect a disappearance of some phases or appearance of some new phases in some nodes (e.g., by dissolution or precipitation of solids). After this "chemistry" run over all nodes is finished, the next time step of the FMT algorithm can be done for the time point t+1, using the updated concentrations of mobile species (also pH, Eh, f(H2), ... ) in aqueous and/or gas phases at time t. The FMT part redistributes matter between nodes (under mass concervation) thus producing corrected compositions of reactive part of each node. So, the coupled RMT algorithm actually runs over all nodes twice at the time point t:  once doing a mass transport iteration using node compositions obtained earlier (at t-1), and then  by applying the "chemical black box" to each node at time t, producing the updated speciation which will be used by the FMT part at the next time point  t+1.

This idea is not really new and it has been implemented in a number of coupled codes (PHREEQC, RockFlow, MCOTAC, ....). These codes use different mass transport algorithms (for advection, diffusion, advection and diffusion), but in most cases the same type of chemical solver based on the law of mass action (LMA) and Newton-Raphson method for minimizing the mass balance deviations. Recently, another type of chemical solvers based on Gibbs energy minimization (GEM) come into coupling with the FMT codes.

Both LMA and GEM can solve the so-called  forward chemical modeling problem which consists in finding the concentrations (or amounts) and activities of chemical species in aqueous and other phases present in a complex chemical system in an equilibrium state defined by temperature T, pressure P, and the system bulk composition b. In practice, calculations for solving such problems range from trivial to enormously complex, depending on the number of species, phases, and stoichiometry units. The complexity further increases when several phases-solutions with redox sensitive components are included in the mass balance; a typical case is the Aq-SS system with the gas mixture phase. Even more complex systems can be considered, e.g. those involving (ad)sorption on several co-existing (stable or metastable) sorbents.

Chemical speciation problems are usually solved with help of the LMA codes, supported with tools such as calculation of Lippmann functions and saturation indexes. A good introduction into modern LMA chemical speciation numerical methods can be found in several textbooks [Van Zeggeren and Storey, 1970; Morel and Hering, 1993; Bethke, 1996] and in the manuals of speciation codes such as MINEQL or PHREEQC [Parkhurst and Appelo, 1999].

The advantages of LMA - excellent mass balance accuracy and fast convergence - are best revealed in the case of simple aqueous speciation models with known stable pure solid phase assemblage and known Eh and fugacities of gases. The LMA codes require thermodynamic data only for the product species (usually aqueous/surface complexes, minerals, and gases), but not for the master species (usually aqueous ions) - this "input data economy" can also be viewed as an advantage.

However, solving with LMA code the complex equilibria involving the aqueous electrolyte phase, many possible condensed phases including solid solutions or fluids is, in general, an inefficient and awkward process because a single aqueous speciation calculation turns into a series of many runs controlled by non-rigorous test procedures for selecting (over)saturated solids one-by-one [see Read, 1982; Bethke, 1996], without a theoretical guarantee of convergence to a unique result. This kind of retardation is difficult to evaluate, but, with a large list of possible condensed phases, more than 5 to 10 LMA sub-runs can be necessary to include all stable solids into mass balance. Only ideal solid solutions or non-ideal binary solid solutions can be included into this LMA iteration scheme.

In the complementary GEM chemical solver setup [for details, see Karpov et al., 1997; 2001; Kulik et al., 2004], the total mole amounts of chemical elements ni and (zero) charge nz comprise the input bulk composition of the chemical system expressed using n(N) independent component (IC). All  n(L) stoichiometrically feasible chemical species or dependent components (DC) belonging to a set L are taken into the mass balance through their chemical formulae and mole amounts nj, j from L. A dependent component belongs to a single- or multi-component aqueous, gaseous, fluid, liquid, solid, or sorption phase. The stability of j-th chemical species at temperature T and pressure P of interest is defined by its input standard molar Gibbs energy function gT,P. Activities and concentrations of dependent components are treated separately in each phase (through subsets of indexes La, a from F), taking into account the appropriate standard and reference states, concentration scales, and mixing models.

Conversely, GEM algorithms can solve complex aqueous - solid (solution) Aq-SS equilibria in one GEM run, in a straightforward way, without any supporting tools such as Lippmann functions and semi-empirical iterative procedures required in LMA Aq-SS speciation models. The applicability of GEM to chemical systems is limited only by the knowledge of standard-state molar properties of end-members and the non-ideal interaction parameters for each phase.

The disadvantages of GEM in comparison with LMA codes are (i) slower convergence (up to 7 times) in simple aqueous speciation calculations and (ii) less mass balance accuracy (1e-7 mol compared to 1e-19 mol in LMA). The performance of GEM can be dramatically improved (7-10 times) using the previous equilibrium speciation as an initial approximation for the new run, if the chemical composition has been changed only slightly. The mass balance accuracy can be improved up to 1e-17 mol (comparable to that of LMA)  at the cost of a few additional iterations (the IPM-2 algorithm), as described in [Chudnenko ea, 2002].

However, performance and chemical feasibility of GEM in complex Aq-SS multiphase systems is unmatched and may be much better than that of LMA codes (though no direct benchmarking is yet available). Another advantage of GEM method (in "convex programming" variant) is that it yields two solutions - the "primal" one for speciation (analogous to LMA) and the "dual" one for chemical potentials of stoichiometry units (not available from LMA). This dual solution opens innovative ways to constructing new mass transport models for diffusion-dominated systems, since at the most general level, the diffusion of any component is driven by the gradient of its chemical potential.    

The present program GEMIPM2K is an implementation of GEM IPM-2 chemical solver with the data exchange interface which makes it possible to couple any existing FMT code with the GEM  "chemical black box" to produce a coupled modeling code with high chemical plausibility. This kind of coupling, in which the chemical information for all nodes is stored in the mass transport part which calls the chemical solver for each node one-by-one, is supported by the TNode C++ class (and its fortran interface). When a new FMT code is written, the whole data storage for all nodes can be organized using a TNodeArray C++ class into two node arrays (for time t and t-1), with each node accessible from the GEM IPM-2 routines or from the FMT program.

This document describes the structure of GEMIPM2K program and the data exchange interface (DATACH and DATABR structures), followed by an overview of TNode and TNodeArray classes and examples of their usage.
